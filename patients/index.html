The Gap

Two people get the same diagnosis on the same day.

One is open by nature. Voices what she feels, what she needs, what she's afraid of, without hesitation. She's almost never alone. Surrounded by people ready to listen, who check in on her daily, loved ones who take leave to accompany her to every appointment. She happens to have a friend who's been through it before. A doctor who shared her WhatsApp number, reachable any time she's afraid.

The other is private by nature. Carries her fear quietly, how she's carried everything: on her own, without asking, without wanting to “trouble” anyone. Mostly alone, she holds herself to unreal thresholds, enduring how she's endured most other things, not knowing where to draw the line. Her doctor does the best possible within brief scheduled consults.

Both are doing everything ‘right’, the best way they naturally would.

One happens to have the ‘right’ personality, the ‘right’ actively engaged support, the ‘right’ doctor.

The other is doing her best too, yet unknowingly slipping through a gap that she and her loved ones didn't see until they hit breaking point.

Right now, which path you get to be on isn't something you chose. Your personality. Who happens to be around you. Which doctor you happen to get.

None of it was chosen. Therein the gap.

Behind Kiin

It took thirty years to appreciate what science alone cannot fix.

I started as a biology and engineering student who became a scientist, convinced that science was the path to solving almost any problem, including curing disease. Working across scientific research, biomedical devices, commercial and clinical systems, I came to understand how complex and human the road is from discovery to solutions that truly reach and work for patients and families.

The consequences hit me at home.
My mom had cancer.

We saw her put on a brave front, then collapse behind it. We celebrated her delayed but successful surgery, after which regular follow-ups over the next twelve months flagged nothing. She collapsed one day and only then did we discover that the cancer had spread widely.

She passed. What hit me was not only the loss, but the realisation that we had all been out of our depth as caregivers. We exhausted all we had, grasping at the wrong straws.

Everyone’s experience is unique. But the pattern is not: patients carry things they can’t find a reason or way to say, families hold knowledge they don’t know is worth sharing, systems treat diseases while whole persons fall through cracks.

Kiin is my commitment to help bridge that gap. I believe that the lived realities of patients and families hold critical insights that need to be surfaced earlier and understood more fully to translate into better care and outcomes.

— Founder, Kiin
